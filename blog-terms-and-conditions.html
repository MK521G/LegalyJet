Do You Need Terms & Conditions? A Plain-English Guide for Website Owners

üìÖ January 2026 ‚è±Ô∏è 7 min read ‚úçÔ∏è LegalyJet Editorial

Terms & Conditions aren't legally required in the way a Privacy Policy is ‚Äî but not having them leaves your business exposed to disputes, abuse, and legal liability that could easily have been avoided.

What are Terms & Conditions?

Terms & Conditions (sometimes called Terms of Service, Terms of Use, or simply "Terms") is a legal agreement between you ‚Äî the owner of a website, app, or online service ‚Äî and the people who use it. When a user visits your site or uses your app, they agree to abide by your Terms & Conditions.

Think of them as the rulebook for your platform. They define what users can and can't do, what rights they have, what happens if something goes wrong, and how disputes will be resolved. Without Terms & Conditions, all of these questions are left unanswered ‚Äî which is a problem for you, not your users.

Are Terms & Conditions legally required?

Unlike a Privacy Policy, Terms & Conditions are generally not legally required by specific privacy laws. However, they are:

  • Required by app stores: Apple's App Store and Google Play require apps to have Terms of Service
  • Required by payment processors: If you accept payments via Stripe, PayPal, or similar services, their agreements require you to have your own Terms
  • Strongly expected by users: The absence of Terms & Conditions often signals an unprofessional or untrustworthy site
  • Practically essential for legal protection: Without them, you have little recourse against user abuse, and your liability exposure is much higher
‚ö†Ô∏è The risk of no Terms & Conditions

Without Terms & Conditions, users can argue that no contract exists governing their use of your platform. This makes it far harder to terminate abusive accounts, enforce restrictions on your content, or limit your liability when something goes wrong.

What do Terms & Conditions protect you from?

Well-drafted Terms & Conditions can protect your business in several important ways:

Intellectual property

Your Terms should state clearly that you own the content on your site ‚Äî your text, images, code, brand, and design ‚Äî and that users cannot copy or reproduce it without permission. If users can submit content (comments, uploads, reviews), your Terms should address who owns that content and what license you have to use it.

Limitation of liability

This is one of the most important clauses. A limitation of liability clause restricts how much financial responsibility you bear if something goes wrong ‚Äî if your service is unavailable, if a user suffers a loss while using your platform, or if content on your site turns out to be inaccurate. Without this clause, your potential liability is unlimited.

Acceptable use

Clearly define what users can and cannot do on your platform. This gives you the legal basis to terminate accounts, remove content, and take action against users who violate your rules.

Dispute resolution

Specify how disputes will be handled ‚Äî through arbitration, in which jurisdiction, and under which governing law. This can significantly reduce the cost of legal disputes if they arise.

Refunds and payments

If your site accepts payments, your Terms must clearly state your payment terms, refund policy, and what happens in the event of a payment dispute. Clear terms here can prevent most chargebacks before they happen.

What should your Terms & Conditions include?

A comprehensive Terms & Conditions document typically covers these sections:

  • Agreement to Terms ‚Äî Establishing that use of your site constitutes agreement to your Terms
  • Description of your service ‚Äî What you offer and what it does
  • User accounts ‚Äî Registration requirements, account responsibilities, and what happens to inactive or banned accounts
  • Acceptable use policy ‚Äî What users can and cannot do
  • Intellectual property ‚Äî Ownership of your content and any user-generated content
  • Payment and billing (if applicable) ‚Äî Pricing, subscription terms, refund policy
  • Disclaimer of warranties ‚Äî "As-is" service provision
  • Limitation of liability ‚Äî Cap on your financial exposure
  • Indemnification ‚Äî User responsibility for their own actions on your platform
  • Governing law and dispute resolution ‚Äî Which jurisdiction's laws apply
  • Termination ‚Äî Your right to terminate accounts
  • Changes to the Terms ‚Äî How and when you can update them
  • Contact information ‚Äî How users can reach you

Terms & Conditions for different types of sites

E-commerce and online stores

Online stores need comprehensive Terms covering product descriptions, pricing, order acceptance, shipping, returns, and refunds. Consumer protection laws in many countries also impose minimum requirements ‚Äî such as cooling-off periods ‚Äî that your Terms must reflect.

SaaS and subscription products

Software-as-a-service products need Terms that cover subscription billing cycles, what happens when payment fails, data handling and backups, uptime commitments (or disclaimers about uptime), and what users can do with your API or data exports.

Content and media platforms

If users can post content ‚Äî comments, reviews, uploads, forum posts ‚Äî you need clear Terms about what content is allowed, how reported content is handled, your DMCA compliance process, and the license users grant you to display their content.

Mobile apps

Mobile app Terms need to address in-app purchases, how the app handles device permissions, and how app updates might change functionality. Both Apple and Google require apps to have Terms of Service, and the content must align with their platform guidelines.

Blogs and informational sites

Even a simple blog needs Terms covering the accuracy of your content (especially if you give advice), affiliate marketing disclosures, comment policies, and intellectual property ownership. If your blog is monetized, your Terms should also address advertising relationships.

Where to display your Terms & Conditions

For Terms & Conditions to be legally enforceable, users must have a reasonable opportunity to read them before agreeing to them. Best practice:

  • Link from your website footer on every page
  • Present during account registration with a checkbox ("I agree to the Terms & Conditions")
  • Reference during checkout and before any purchase
  • Include in your app store listing
  • Include in any email communications about service changes

A "browsewrap" agreement ‚Äî where simply visiting a site constitutes agreement ‚Äî is increasingly hard to enforce in court. A "clickwrap" agreement ‚Äî where users actively check a box or click "I agree" ‚Äî is much stronger. Use clickwrap wherever possible for important actions.

Updating your Terms & Conditions

Like your Privacy Policy, your Terms & Conditions should be reviewed whenever your business changes ‚Äî new features, new payment methods, changes to your acceptable use policy, or changes in the laws that apply to you. Always update the "Last updated" date, and notify existing users of material changes.

The bottom line

Terms & Conditions are one of the most powerful tools you have to protect your business online. They establish the rules of engagement, limit your liability, and give you recourse when users violate your policies. Without them, you're operating on good faith ‚Äî and good faith doesn't hold up in court.
